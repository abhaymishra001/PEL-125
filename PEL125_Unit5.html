✍️ Detailed Handwritten Notes
📢 2.1 Direct Speech vs Reported (Indirect) Speech
📌 Direct Speech

The exact words spoken by a person, enclosed in quotation marks (" "). Uses reporting verbs like said, asked, exclaimed.

Example: Riya said, "I am preparing for my exam."
📌 Reported (Indirect) Speech

Reporting someone's words without quotation marks. Verb tense, pronouns, and time expressions change.

Example: Riya said that she was preparing for her exam.
Feature Direct Speech Reported Speech
Quotation Marks ✅ Yes ❌ No
Tense As spoken Backshifted
Pronouns As spoken Changed (SON rule)
Time expressions As spoken Changed
Question mark ✅ If question ❌ No

Direct speech uses Double quotes → Direct. Reported speech Removes quotes and Reports the meaning.

💡 Why Do We Need Reported Speech?

In real life, we report conversations rather than quoting them exactly. Used in:

  • News reporting — journalists relay statements
  • Storytelling — narrating past conversations
  • Academic writing — citing sources indirectly
  • Daily conversations — "He told me that..."
⏰ 2.2 Change of Tense (Backshifting)
🔥 Most asked topic in exams! Learn all tense changes.

When the reporting verb is in the past tense (said, told, asked), the tense of the reported clause shifts one step back into the past.

Why? Teacher: "I will check your homework tomorrow."
Next day → The teacher said she would check our homework the next day.
(will → would | tomorrow → the next day)
Direct Speech Tense → Reported Speech Tense
Present Simple (play) → Past Simple (played)
Present Continuous (is playing) → Past Continuous (was playing)
Present Perfect (has played) → Past Perfect (had played)
Present Perfect Continuous → Past Perfect Continuous
Past Simple (played) → Past Simple / Past Perfect
Past Continuous (was playing) → Past Perfect Continuous
will / shall → would / should
can / may → could / might
is / am / are → was / were
was / were → had been
must → must / had to
⚠️ When tense does NOT change
  • When the statement is a universal truth → He said, "The sun rises in the east." → He said that the sun rises in the east.
  • When the reporting verb is in present or future tense

"SHIFT BACK ONE STEP" — Simple → Simple Past → Perfect. Think of it as going one floor down in a building: Present → Past → Past Perfect.

🕐 2.3 Change in Time & Place Expressions

Time and place words change because the context of speech has shifted to a different time and place.

Direct Speech → Reported Speech
now → then
today → that day
tonight → that night
yesterday → the previous day / the day before
last night → the previous night / the night before
tomorrow → the next day / the following day
ago → before
this / these → that / those
here → there
thus → so
just → then

"THIS → THAT, HERE → THERE, NOW → THEN" — Proximity words move away because the speech event is now in the past. Think: what was CLOSE (this/here/now) becomes FAR (that/there/then).

👤 2.4 Change of Pronouns — The SON Rule
🔑 The SON Rule (Easy Memory Trick!)
  • S — 1st person pronouns change to match the Subject of reporting clause
  • O — 2nd person pronouns change to match the Object of reporting clause
  • N — 3rd person pronouns show No change
Direct Speech → Reported Speech
I → he / she
you → I / he / she / we / they (context dependent)
we → they
me → him / her
you (obj) → him / her / us / them
us → them
my → his / her
your → my / his / her / our / their
our → their
mine → his / hers
yours → mine / his / hers / ours / theirs
"You are stupid!" — Three possible reports:
• She said that I was stupid. (if said to the reporter)
• She said that we were stupid. (if said to a group)
• She said that he was stupid. (if said to a third person)
🗣️ 2.5 Reporting Verbs — More Than Just 'Said'

Using varied reporting verbs makes reported speech more precise and natural.

For Statements
  • said, told, stated, explained, added, mentioned, claimed, admitted, confessed, announced, declared
For Questions
  • asked, inquired, wanted to know, wondered, queried
For Requests / Commands
  • requested, begged, ordered, commanded, instructed, advised, warned, urged, reminded
For Offers
  • offered, promised, agreed, volunteered, proposed, suggested
❌ Weak: He said, "Please sit down." → He said me to sit down.
✅ Better: He requested me to sit down.
❓ 2.6 Reported Questions

Reported questions are rewritten as statements, not as questions.

⚠️ Key Rules for Reported Questions
  • No question mark ( ? )
  • No inversion of subject and verb (not "asked where was he" — wrong!)
  • Use reporting verbs: asked, inquired, wanted to know
Type 1: Yes/No Questions

Structure: asked + if / whether + subject + verb

Direct: She asked, "Are you ready?"
Reported: She asked if I was ready.

Direct: He said, "Did she pass the exam?"
Reported: He asked whether she had passed the exam.
Type 2: Wh- Questions

Structure: asked + wh-word + subject + verb (NO if/whether)

Direct: He asked, "Where do you work?"
Reported: He asked where I worked.

Direct: She asked, "What time will you arrive?"
Reported: She asked what time I would arrive.
Question Type Connector Used Example
Yes/No if / whether asked if she was ready
What / Which what / which asked what I wanted
Where / When where / when asked where I lived
Who / Whom who / whom asked who had called
Why / How why / how asked why I was late

YES/NO → IF. Easy: if the question has no question word, use IF. If it has a question word (Wh-), use that word directly. No "if" with Wh- words!

🙏 2.7 Reported Requests

Requests are polite commands. They are reported using to / not to + base verb.

Structure

Reporting verb + object + to / not to + base verb

Direct Speech → Reported Speech
"Please wait outside." → She requested me to wait outside.
"Open your books." → The teacher instructed them to open their books.
"Don't make noise." → She warned them not to make noise.
"Please help me." → She requested me to help her.
"Show your ID card." → The officer ordered him to show his ID card.
🔑 Choosing the Right Verb for Requests
  • requested — polite ask ("Please help me")
  • ordered / commanded — authoritative command ("Submit the file!")
  • begged — desperate request ("Please, please help me!")
  • advised — giving advice ("You should rest.")
  • warned — warning ("Don't touch that!")
  • instructed — giving instructions ("Open your books.")
🤝 2.8 Reported Offers

Offers express willingness to help. They use the structure: offered / promised + to + base verb.

Direct Speech → Reported Speech
"I will help you with the project." → He offered to help me with the project.
"I can drop you home." → She offered to drop me home.
"Shall I help you with homework?" → He offered to help me with my homework.
"Shall we start the meeting?" → Ram suggested starting the meeting then.
"May I open the window?" → She offered to open the window for me.

Offer keywords: "Shall I…?", "I will…", "Can I…?", "I could…" all signal offers. Use offered to for most. Use suggested + gerund (-ing) for group suggestions like "Shall we…?"

📝 2.9 Worked Examples (Activity Solutions)
Tense & Time Changes
1. Rohan said, "I met my teacher yesterday." → Rohan said that he had met his teacher the previous day.
2. She said, "I will call you tomorrow." → She said that she would call me the next day.
3. He said, "I completed the task two hours ago." → He said that he had completed the task two hours before.
4. She said, "These books are mine." → She said that those books were hers.
5. The guide said, "We will stay here." → The guide said that they would stay there.
Pronoun Changes
1. She said, "I am very tired." → She said that she was very tired.
2. Ravi said to me, "You are my best friend." → Ravi told me that I was his best friend.
3. They said, "We will complete our work." → They said that they would complete their work.
4. My mother said to me, "You should take care of your health." → My mother advised me that I should take care of my health.
5. She said to him, "I trust you." → She told him that she trusted him.
Mixed Practice
1. He said, "I have got a toothache." → He said that he had got a toothache.
2. Manu said, "I am very busy now." → Manu said that he was very busy then.
3. "Hurry up," she said to us. → She urged us to hurry up.
4. "Give me a cup of water," he told her. → He asked her to give him a cup of water.
5. She said to me, "Thank you." → She thanked me.
6. Raju said, "Gautam must go tomorrow." → Raju said that Gautam must/had to go the next day.
7. Geetha says, "My father is an Engineer." → Geetha says that her father is an Engineer. (present reporting verb → no backshift!)
8. 'Where are you going?' James asked Mary. → James asked Mary where she was going.

🔑 Key Definitions (Exam Ready)
  • Direct Speech: The exact words of the speaker enclosed in quotation marks. E.g., He said, "I am tired."
  • Reported Speech: Repeating someone's words without quotation marks, with grammatical changes. E.g., He said that he was tired.
  • Backshifting: Moving the tense of the reported clause one step back in time.
  • Reporting Verb: The verb that introduces the reported speech (said, asked, told, ordered, etc.).
  • SON Rule: 1st person → Subject, 2nd person → Object, 3rd person → No change.
  • Reported Question: A question rewritten as a statement with no question mark and no subject-verb inversion.
  • Reported Request: A polite command reported using to/not to + base verb.
  • Reported Offer: An expression of willingness, reported using offered to + base verb.
