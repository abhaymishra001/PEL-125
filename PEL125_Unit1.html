✍️ Section 2: Detailed Handwritten Notes ▼
🔵 1. Personal Pronouns
📖 Definition: A pronoun is a word that replaces a noun to avoid repetition. Personal pronouns refer to specific persons or things.
  • Two forms: Subject Form (does the action) and Object Form (receives the action)
Person Subject (Singular) Subject (Plural) Object (Singular) Object (Plural)
1st I we me us
2nd you you you you
3rd he/she/it they him/her/it them
✅ She forgot the meeting. (Subject) | I haven't seen her today. (Object)
Subject pronouns = I, we feel important (they come first!). Object pronouns = me, us receive things!
Special Uses: THERE+BE (existence): "There is a phone box" | IT = time/weather/distance/situation: "It's raining" | YOU/ONE = generic person | THEY = people in general
🌸 2. Reflexive Pronouns
📖 Definition: Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject — when the subject and object are the same person.
Person Singular Plural
1st myself ourselves
2nd yourself yourselves
3rd himself/herself/itself themselves
  • When to use: Subject acts on itself → Mark made himself a sandwich
  • After prepositions (most): Look after themselves
  • To emphasize: I did it myself
Common Mistake: ❌ "The gunman shot him" (wrong if subject=object) → ✅ "The gunman shot himself" | BUT after PLACE prepositions: "Behind me" (NOT myself) — use regular pronouns!
🔧 Tech Writing: "The system resets" (not resets itself) | "The algorithm adapts itself to new data" (for emphasis only)
⚠️ Verbs that DON'T use Reflexive:
affordconcentratedeciderelaxrememberhurrymeetrestwake upsit downwondercomplain
Common Idioms: enjoy yourself = have a good time | help yourself = take what you want | by myself = alone/on my own
🔄 3. Reciprocal Pronouns
📖 Definition: Reciprocal pronouns show mutual action between two or more people — A does X to B, and B does the same to A.
  • Only two: each other (2 people) and one another (3+ people) — but used interchangeably today!
  • They respect one another's opinions | They don't like each other
  • Possessive form: use apostrophe → each other's phone numbers
Each Other (Mutual) Themselves (Individual)
They're laughing at each other
(A at B, B at A)		 They're laughing at themselves
(each person at themselves)
EACH OTHER = mutual exchange. Think of two mirrors facing each other! 🪞🪞
🔄 4. Substitute Pronouns
📖 Definition: Replace nouns to avoid repetition. Main substitutes: one, ones, so, not
  • ONE = replaces a singular countable noun → "I need a pen. Do you have one?"
  • ONES = replaces plural countable nouns → "I like the blue shirts, not the white ones."
  • SO = replaces affirmative clause → "Will it work?" → "I hope so"
  • NOT = replaces negative clause → "I hope not" / "I'm afraid not"
Question Affirmative (SO) Negative (NOT)
Will it work? I hope so I hope not
Is she ready? I think so I don't think so
Can you attend? I expect so I'm afraid not
DON'T USE one/ones with: Uncountable nouns ("some one" ❌) | Possessives alone ("my one" ❌) | After numbers alone ("three ones" ❌)
DO USE with: Adjectives ("the red one" ✅) | this/that/these/those | which
💥 5. Emphatic Pronouns
📖 Definition: Same form as reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, etc.) but used for emphasis or to show independent/unaided action.
  • Use 1 – Without Help: "I built this boat myself" = without anyone's help
  • Use 2 – Special Emphasis: "The manager himself welcomed us" = the manager personally, not staff
  • Position: after the noun/pronoun it relates to
  • EMPHATIC = OPTIONAL — can be removed without changing basic meaning
Reflexive (NECESSARY) Emphatic (OPTIONAL)
"She hurt herself"
❌ Cannot be removed		 "She herself did it"
✅ Can be removed
REFLEXIVE = necessary for meaning (like a mirror, essential) | EMPHATIC = optional stress (like bold text — adds intensity!) 💪
📐 Engineering Context: "The CEO himself approved the design" | "We tested the software ourselves" | "The algorithm itself is efficient"
⚡ 6. Adverbs — Definition & Modification
📖 Definition: An adverb modifies verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. It tells HOW, WHEN, WHERE, or TO WHAT EXTENT an action happens.
🏃
Modifies VERB
She ran quickly
Comes after verb/object
✨
Modifies ADJECTIVE
The proposal was exceptionally comprehensive
Comes BEFORE adjective
🔥
Modifies ADVERB
She ran very quickly
Comes BEFORE other adverb
📊 7. Types of Adverbs
Memory: M-P-T-F-D → Manner, Place, Time, Frequency, Degree → "My Parrot Talks Fast Daily" 🦜
Type Function Key Examples Sentence Example
Manner HOW something happens quickly, carefully, politely, meticulously He ran quickly.
Place WHERE it happens here, there, outside, nearby, abroad They met outside.
Time WHEN it happens now, soon, yesterday, today, already We'll leave soon.
Frequency HOW OFTEN it happens always, usually, often, rarely, never She always smiles.
Degree TO WHAT EXTENT very, quite, extremely, rather, too, highly It's very cold.
📍 8. Position of Adverbs
Adverbs can appear in 3 positions: Front (beginning), Mid (middle), End (end of sentence)
📌 FRONT Position
Adverb at start, usually sentence adverbs for emphasis

Undoubtedly, she already submitted the paper.
Remarkably, the team finished.
Historically, this region...
🎯 MID Position
Between subject and verb (or after auxiliary verb)

He frequently travels abroad.
I have rarely seen this.
She will certainly address it.
🔚 END Position
After the verb or object — most common

Tom ate his breakfast quickly.
We played volleyball yesterday.
She arrived with great precision.
VERB + OBJECT Rule: Adverb does NOT go between verb and direct object!
❌ "Tom ate quickly his breakfast" → ✅ "Tom ate his breakfast quickly"
❌ "We played yesterday volleyball" → ✅ "We played volleyball yesterday"
EXCEPTION: Adverb CAN go before a long object → "Detectives examined carefully the contents of the dead man's pockets."
🔗 9. Sentence Adverbs & Linking Words
📖 Sentence Adverbs: Modify the whole sentence, not a single word. Express the speaker's attitude.
  • Examples: Fortunately, certainly, perhaps, of course, probably, undoubtedly
  • Can go in front, mid, or end position
  • Fortunately, the weather stayed fine. | We probably won't get there in time.
  • Also → mid position | too / as well → end position
📖 Linking Words (Conjunctive Adverbs): Join ideas between clauses or sentences.
  • Examples: however, therefore, moreover, consequently, nevertheless, furthermore
  • Often used in formal writing and academic reports
  • She was tired; however, she finished her work.
Function Linking Words
Contrast however, nevertheless, on the other hand
Cause/Result therefore, consequently, thus, hence
Addition moreover, furthermore, in addition, also
Sequence firstly, subsequently, finally, then
