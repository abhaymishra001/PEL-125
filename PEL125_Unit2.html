✏️ Detailed Handwritten Notes
📍 Direct Objects (DO)
Formula: Subject + Verb + Direct Object
  • The direct object is the thing/person that the verb's action is performed on.
  • Ask "What?" or "Whom?" after the verb → that answer is the direct object.
  • ONLY action verbs can have direct objects. Linking verbs (am, is, are, was, were) cannot.
  • A direct object is NEVER an adverb or the object of a preposition.
📝 Examples
The company launched a new digital platform. → DO = platform
The manager approved the proposal. → DO = proposal
The message reached the manager. → DO = manager
His landlord is raising the rent. → DO = rent
To find DO: Ask "Subject Verb WHAT?" The answer = Direct Object!
Sentence Verb Direct Object
She bought flowers. bought flowers ✅
He runs quickly. runs ❌ (no DO)
They reviewed the code. reviewed the code ✅
She is happy. is (linking) ❌ (linking verb)
Key Practice Answers: 1→b) the proposal   2→c) his advice   3→c) valuable data
📍 Indirect Objects (IO)
Formula: Subject + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object
  • The indirect object tells us TO WHOM or FOR WHOM the action is done.
  • It appears between the verb and direct object.
  • Can be rewritten using a prepositional phrase with "to" or "for".
  • Common verbs that take IO: give send show tell teach bring make buy lend offer
  • ⚠️ If "to/for + noun" = prepositional phrase → NOT an indirect object!
📝 Examples
My mom sent me a package. → IO=me, DO=package
I bought her flowers = I bought flowers for her.
She taught us grammar. → IO=us, DO=grammar
The HR manager sent the candidates an offer letter.
Indirect Object = the RECIPIENT. Ask "Verb DO to WHOM / for WHOM?" → that = IO
Sentence IO? Reason
The manager gave the team clear instructions. ✅ TRUE team receives instructions
She explained the procedure to the interns. ❌ FALSE prepositional phrase, not IO
The professor offered a detailed explanation to class. ❌ FALSE it is the Direct Object
They sent their clients the final report. ✅ TRUE clients receive the report
The trainer promised the participants sessions. ✅ TRUE participants are promised something
❓ Yes/No Questions
  • Can be answered with only "yes" or "no".
  • Always begin with an auxiliary verb (do, does, did, is, are, was, were, have, has, can, will, etc.).
Rule 1 (be verbs): Invert subject & verb
Rule 2 (action verbs): Add do/does/did before subject
Rule 3 (modals): Invert modal & subject
📝 Examples
Be verb: Is the project aligned with company goals?
Action verb: Did the client approve the proposal?
Modal: Can you swim?

Speaking Activity Questions:
• Have you completed a group project recently?
• Are you comfortable speaking in English during interviews?
• Did you face any challenges in online learning?
• Can you adapt to a fast-paced work environment?
Quick Tip: If statement has auxiliary → MOVE it to front. If not → ADD do/does/did!
❓ Wh- Questions
  • Used to get specific information, not just yes/no answers.
  • Wh-words: What When Where Why Who Whom Whose Which How
WH-word + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb
WH-word Asks about Example
What thing/object What is this? / What time is it?
Why reason Why were you late?
Who person (subject) Who is that woman?
When time/occasion When did you arrive?
How manner/way How do you go to school?
How much quantity (uncountable) How much homework?
How many quantity (countable) How many books?
Which choice Which country? / Which device?
Whom person (object) Whom did you meet?
Whose possession Whose resume was selected?
📝 Fill-in-the-blank Answers
1-Why · 2-Who · 3-Whom · 4-When · 5-Which · 6-How · 7-Which · 8-Whose · 9-What · 10-What
WH-questions give DETAILED answers → Very important in interviews and professional communication!
🔀 Subject vs. Object Questions
Subject Question (SQ) Object Question (OQ)
Asks about the subject (WHO does the action) Asks about the object (WHOM receives the action)
Does NOT use do/does/did DOES use do/does/did
Structure: WH + V + (rest) Structure: WH + do/does/did + S + V
Who led the team? (No did!) Whom did the company appoint?
What caused the failure? What did you discuss?
📝 Practice Answers
1→ b) Who completed… (SQ)  |  2→ b) Who did you contact… (OQ)
3→ a) Which student won… (SQ)  |  4→ a) What caused… (SQ)
5→ a) Who did the interviewer select… (OQ)  |  6→ b) Which company offered… (SQ)
TRICK: If WH-word IS the subject → no did/does/do needed. If WH-word is the OBJECT → must add do/does/did!
📌 Prepositions in Wh-questions
  • A preposition can come at the END (informal) OR before the Wh-word (formal).
Informal (Preposition at end) Formal (Preposition before Wh-word)
Who are you talking to? To whom are you talking?
What are you looking for? For what are you searching?
Where did she come from? (From where did she come?)
Which city do you live in? In which city do you live?
Informal → preposition at END (everyday use). Formal → preposition BEFORE wh-word (exams, writing).
🚫 Negative Statements
  • A sentence stating something is NOT true or did not happen.
  • Common negative words: not never no nobody nothing hardly rarely barely
With Auxiliary: Subject + Auxiliary + not + Verb
Without Auxiliary: Subject + do/does/did + not + base verb
📝 Examples
She is not working today. (auxiliary = is)
They have not arrived yet. (auxiliary = have)
I do not like coffee. (no auxiliary → add do)
She did not finish the exam. (past → add did)
❓ Negative Questions
  • Used to: express surprise, confirm information, or make polite suggestions.
Auxiliary + n't + Subject + Verb?
📝 Examples (convert to negative questions)
"They have informed the client about the delay." → Haven't they informed the client about the delay?
"Quality should be our top priority." → Shouldn't quality be our top priority?
"You have submitted the file." → Haven't you submitted the file?
🤝 So / Neither Do I Statements
SO (Positive Agreement) NEITHER (Negative Agreement)
Use with positive statements Use with negative statements
Structure: So + Aux/Modal + Subject Structure: Neither + Aux/Modal + Subject
Tom has gone → So has Nick File hasn't been backed up → Neither has the other one
United won → So did Rangers Emma isn't here → Neither/Nor is Matthew
We're overloaded → So are we Doesn't support update → Neither does this version
POSITIVE statement → use SO  |  NEGATIVE statement → use NEITHER/NOR
Always match the auxiliary from the original sentence!
💭 I Think So Statements
  • Expresses personal opinion, belief, or uncertainty.
  • Use "so" after: be afraid believe expect guess hope suppose think
  • ⚠️ In negative: put "not" with the opinion verb, NOT the following verb.
Structure: I think + (that) + clause  |  Positive: I hope so.  |  Negative: I hope not.
📝 Examples
Will the team meet the deadline? → I expect so. / I don't expect so.
Is there a risk of delays? → I'm afraid so. / I'm afraid not.
Will the proposal receive funding? → I hope so. / I hope not.
⚠️ "I don't think so." (NOT "I think not so.") — negation goes with the opinion verb!

📋 Quick Revision Sheet
⚡ CHEAT SHEET — Unit II: Statements & Questions

🔵 Direct Object

• What/whom the verb acts on
• Formula: S + V + DO
• Ask "What?" or "Whom?" after verb
• Only with action verbs
• NOT adverb/prep phrase
• Ex: She bought flowers

🩷 Indirect Object

• Recipient of the action
• Formula: S + V + IO + DO
• Sits between verb and DO
• = "to/for + noun" prepositional form
• Verbs: give, send, teach, show…
• Ex: She gave us worksheets

💚 Yes/No Questions

• Answer: yes or no only
• Be verb → invert S and V
• Action verb → add do/does/did
• Modal → invert modal + S
• Ex: Did she finish? / Can you come?

💜 Wh- Questions

• What, When, Where, Why, Who, Whom, Whose, Which, How
• Structure: WH + Aux + S + V
• Give detailed info answers
• Used in interviews
• Ex: Why did you choose this role?

🟠 Subject vs Object Q

• Subject Q: WH = subject → NO do/did
  "Who called?" (who = subject)
• Object Q: WH = object → ADD do/did
  "Who did you call?" (you = subject)
• KEY: who does the action = subject

🩵 Prepositions in Wh-Q

• Informal → preposition at END
  "Who are you talking to?"
• Formal → preposition BEFORE wh
  "To whom are you talking?"
• Both are grammatically correct!

🟡 Negative Statements

• With aux: S + aux + not + V
  She is NOT working today.
• Without aux: S + do/did + not + V
  I do NOT like coffee.
• neg words: never, nobody, nothing…

🔷 So / Neither / I think so

• POSITIVE → So + aux + S
  "I passed. So did she."
• NEGATIVE → Neither + aux + S
  "I haven't eaten. Neither have they."
• Opinion: I hope so / I hope not
  Neg: "I don't think so." ✅
