This unit covers two major grammar topics:

  • Determiners – words placed before nouns to identify or quantify them (possessive, demonstrative, distributive & quantitative)

  • Modal Verb – auxiliary verb changing meaning
  • Base Form – verb without to/inflection

  • Distributive – individual members of group
  • Quantitative – expresses amount

Detailed Handwritten Notes
🔤 What Are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs are special auxiliary (helping) verbs that express meaning such as ability, permission, possibility, necessity, requests, suggestions, offers, and invitations.


Key Characteristics:

  • Always followed by the base form of the main verb (NO "to")
  • Never change their form (no -s, -ed, or -ing endings)
  • Questions are formed by inverting the modal with the subject
  • Never use two modals together

Common Modals: cancouldmaymightmustshallshouldwillwould
Correct: "I must go." ✅    Wrong: "I must to go." ❌
Correct: "She can swim." ✅    Wrong: "She cans swim." ❌
CAN'T MISS WILL = Can, Cannot, May, Might, Should, Should not, Will, Would = 9 core modals!
🏋️ MODALS OF ABILITY
✅ CAN – Present Ability

Used for present or general ability – something a person is capable of doing right now.

• "I can speak three languages fluently."
• "The student can explain her project using simple English."
• "She can adapt her communication style depending on the audience."
Use "can" when the ability exists at the present moment.
⏰ COULD – Past Ability

Used for past ability (something you were able to do before) or a polite request.

• "By the end of the project, I could confidently present technical solutions."
• "She could explain technical concepts clearly even at an early stage."
• "Could you pass the salt, please?" (polite request)
Past ability → "could" (NOT "can") for past time references!
📅 BE ABLE TO – All Tenses

"Be able to" is used when modals like "can/could" don't work — especially in future tenses, perfect tenses, or after another modal.

• "I will be able to contribute effectively once I complete the training." (future)
• "She has been able to complete the project." (present perfect)
• "I might be able to help you tomorrow." (after another modal)
Use "be able to" in all tenses where "can/could" cannot be used.
🔓 MODALS OF PERMISSION
Modal Type Examples
CAN Informal "Can I sit here?" / "Can we try a different approach?"
MAY Formal "May I seek your approval?" / "Candidates may leave after submitting answer sheets."
COULD Polite Request "Could you consider mentoring me?" / "Could I take a few minutes of your time?"
MIGHT Very Tentative "Might I join you for lunch?" (British English, very formal)
Formality Scale: MIGHT > MAY > COULD > CAN (from most formal to least formal)
🎲 MODALS OF POSSIBILITY
🌤️ MAY – Likely Possibility

Expresses a fairly likely possibility (~50-60% chance)

• "There may be a slight delay in receiving the final approval."
• "This approach may lead to improved customer retention."
🌦️ MIGHT – Less Certain

Expresses a less certain possibility (~30-40% chance)

• "The inconsistency in results might stem from limitations in the experimental design."
• "There might be a slight change in the meeting agenda."
❓ COULD – Possible but Uncertain + MUST – Logical Certainty

COULD = possible but uncertain

• "The new policy could alter employee engagement levels."
• "A lack of coordination could lead to missed deadlines."

MUST = strong logical deduction (~100%)

• "He must be the new teacher." (confident deduction from evidence)
• "She's not answering – she must be busy."
📋 NECESSITY & OBLIGATION
Modal Meaning Example Key Difference
MUST Strong necessity / speaker's internal feeling "You must wear a seatbelt." Internal obligation / speaker's strong view
HAVE TO External obligation / rule imposed by others "I have to finish work by 5 PM." (company policy) External rule / circumstance imposed
SHOULD Mild advice / recommendation "You should see a doctor." Suggestion, not mandatory
MUST NOT Prohibition (absolutely not allowed) "You must not smoke here." Prohibition
DON'T HAVE TO No obligation (not necessary but allowed) "You don't have to come." Optional, not prohibited
MUST NOT ≠ DON'T HAVE TO! "Must not" = prohibition. "Don't have to" = no obligation but you can if you want.
🤝 SOCIAL FUNCTIONS (Requests, Suggestions, Offers, Invitations)
Function Modals Used Examples
Requests Will, Would, Can, Could "Would you help me with this?" / "Can you pass the report?"
Suggestions Should, Shall, Might "You should try the new approach." / "Shall we meet at 3 PM?"
Offers Will, Shall, Would "Shall I make coffee?" / "Would you like some help?"
Invitations Shall, Would, Will "Shall we dance?" / "Would you join us for lunch?"
"Would you like some coffee?" (polite offer) vs. "Shall I make coffee?" (offer/suggestion, slightly formal)
⏮️ PAST MODALS
Past Modal Meaning Example
COULD Past ability "She could read at age 4." / "As a child, she could run very fast."
SHOULD HAVE + past participle Regret / past advice not followed "You should have called me." / "We should have planned more carefully."
MIGHT HAVE / MAY HAVE + past participle Past possibility "He might have missed the train." / "She may have forgotten the meeting."
WOULD HAVE + past participle Hypothetical past (unreal condition) "I would have gone if invited." / "They would have succeeded with more time."
COULD HAVE + past participle Past possibility (didn't happen) "She could have won if she had practiced."
MUST HAVE + past participle Logical deduction about past "He must have forgotten – he never misses meetings."
Formula: Modal + HAVE + Past Participle = Past Modal. E.g., "should have gone", "might have eaten", "would have seen"
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
🚫 Don'ts with Modal Verbs
Wrong ❌ Correct ✅ Rule
"She cans swim" "She can swim" No -s with modals
"I must to go" "I must go" No "to" after modal
"might could go" "might go" No double modals
"I can swim as a child" "I could swim as a child" Use "could" for past ability
"May" for possibility "Might" for possibility Especially in American English
🏷️ DETERMINERS
📌 What Are Determiners?
Determiners are words placed before nouns to provide essential context and clarity. They tell us which noun, how many, or who possesses it.

Think of them as the GPS of language — they guide readers to understand exactly what you're talking about.


Common examples: the my this each some


There are 4 types covered in this unit:

  1. Possessive Determiners
  2. Demonstrative Determiners
  3. Distributive Determiners
  4. Quantitative Determiners
🔑 1. POSSESSIVE DETERMINERS
👤 Showing Ownership

Possessive determiners indicate possession, belonging, or relationship. They always come directly before the noun.

Determiner Person Example
my 1st person singular "My coffee has gone cold."
your 2nd person "Your presentation was excellent."
his 3rd person singular (male) "His report is ready."
her 3rd person singular (female) "Her book is on the table."
its 3rd person singular (thing/animal) "The dog wagged its tail."
our 1st person plural "Our project was approved."
their 3rd person plural "Their presentation impressed everyone."
Possessive determiners ≠ possessive pronouns! "my" (determiner) vs "mine" (pronoun). "my book" vs "the book is mine."
👆 2. DEMONSTRATIVE DETERMINERS
📍 Pointing Out Specific Things
Determiner Distance Number Example
THIS Near (close) Singular "This car is fast."
THAT Far (distant) Singular "That mountain is beautiful."
THESE Near (close) Plural "These cookies are delicious."
THOSE Far (distant) Plural "Those houses are old."
TH-IS (singular near) → TH-ESE (plural near) | TH-AT (singular far) → TH-OSE (plural far). Near = IS/ESE | Far = AT/OSE

They can also indicate time: "this morning" (current), "that day" (past), "these days" (current period), "those times" (past period).

🧩 3. DISTRIBUTIVE DETERMINERS
👥 Referring to Individuals in a Group

Distributive determiners break down a group and focus on individual members rather than the group as a whole.

Determiner Meaning Example
EACH Every single one individually (emphasis on one by one) "Each student must submit their assignment."
EVERY All members individually (more general) "Every employee received a bonus."
EITHER One or the other of TWO "Either option works for me."
NEITHER Not one and not the other of TWO "Neither answer is correct."
BOTH The two together "Both candidates are qualified."
EACH = one by one (like counting). EVERY = all together in mind. EITHER/NEITHER/BOTH = always about TWO things!
Each/Every take SINGULAR verb: "Each student IS..." / "Every employee WAS..." | Both takes PLURAL: "Both ARE..."
🔢 4. QUANTITATIVE DETERMINERS
📊 Expressing Amount or Quantity

Quantitative determiners answer: How much? / How many?

Determiner Used With Meaning Example
FEW Countable (plural) Small number (negative – not enough) "Few students passed." (implies a problem)
A FEW Countable (plural) Small number (positive – some exist) "A few students passed." (implies some did)
LITTLE Uncountable Small amount (negative – not enough) "There's little milk." (nearly none)
A LITTLE Uncountable Small amount (positive – some exists) "Add a little sugar." (some, enough)
MANY Countable (plural) Large number "Many friends attended."
MUCH Uncountable Large amount "Not much time left."
SOME Both Moderate, unspecified amount "She has some questions."
SEVERAL Countable More than two but not very many "Several solutions were proposed."
ALL Both Entire quantity "All students passed."
ENOUGH Both Sufficient amount "There is enough food."
FEW/LITTLE = negative connotation (not enough). A FEW/A LITTLE = positive connotation (some, sufficient). The article "A" changes the meaning!
MANY/FEW → Countable Nouns (you can count them: books, students). MUCH/LITTLE → Uncountable Nouns (you can't count: water, time, money).
⚖️ DISTRIBUTIVE vs QUANTITATIVE: Key Difference
🆚 Comparison
Aspect Distributive Quantitative
Focus Individual members Amount/number of group
Emphasis Individuality, separation Size, scale, magnitude
Example "Each student received feedback" (personal) "Many students received feedback" (aggregate)
Implies Fairness, distribution, choice Count, total, approximate amount
Group treatment Separates group members Treats group collectively
